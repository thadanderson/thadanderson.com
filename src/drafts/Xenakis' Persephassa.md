# Inside the Sonic Storm: Xenakis's Persephassa

> [!ABSTRACT] Episode Summary
> *Persephassa* (1969) is considered one of the most significant works in the percussion canon. Premiered in the ruins of Persepolis, Iran, the work utilizes a "surround sound" architecture where the six percussionists encompass the audience.

### 🎼 The Architecture of Sound

The defining characteristic of *Persephassa* is its spatial requirement. The six percussionists are arranged in a hexagon around the audience. This creates unique acoustic challenges:

* **Time Lag:** Players on opposite sides of the setup (e.g., Player 1 and Player 4) can be separated by 80-100 feet.
* **Sound Rotation:** Xenakis utilizes this spacing to create "spatial swirls" where accents are passed from player to player, physically moving the sound around the listener.

### 🥁 Instrumentation Highlights

Xenakis avoids standard melodic instruments (marimba, vibraphone) in favor of elemental sounds.

| Instrument | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Simantras** | Both wood and metal. Originally used in Eastern Orthodox monasteries. |
| **Galets** | Sea stones. Required to have specific pitches relative to one another. |
| **Affolantes** | Sheets of metal (or thundersheets) creating a roaring, dense noise. |

### 💡 Performance Practice: The "Click" Debate

One of the most common questions regarding *Persephassa* is the use of a click track.

> [!question] To Click or Not to Click?
> **Argument for Click:** The complex polyrhythms and spatial distance make audio precision nearly impossible without a reference track. It ensures the mathematical integrity of the score.
>
> **Argument Against Click:** *Les Percussions de Strasbourg* (the ensemble for whom the piece was written) perform without a click. They rely on intense **visual cues** and a shared internal pulse. This creates a more "chamber music" feel and allows the piece to breathe organically.
