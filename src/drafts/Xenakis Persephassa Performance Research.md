Iannis Xenakis (1922–2001) was a Romanian-born Greek-French avant-garde composer, music theorist, architect, and engineer. He is considered one of the most important post-war avant-garde composers and is renowned for pioneering the use of mathematical models and computer-assisted composition in music, such as stochastic processes, set theory, and game theory.

- **Architecture and Engineering:** Trained as a civil engineer, Xenakis worked as an architect and engineer with the renowned architect Le Corbusier in Paris for 12 years after fleeing Greece in 1947. His notable projects include the design of the Philips Pavilion for the 1958 Brussels World's Fair.

- **Musical Innovation:** Encouraged by composer Olivier Messiaen to apply his scientific training to music, Xenakis developed revolutionary compositional techniques. He is particularly known for:
	- **Stochastic Music:** Using the mathematics of probability to create musical compositions.
	- **Electronic and Computer Music:** A key figure in the development of electronic and computer-assisted composition, he created works using his own UPIC system, which allowed users to draw music directly onto a digitizing tablet.
	- **Spatialization:** Experimenting with placing musicians in different locations among the audience to create a spatial sound experience.

- **Multimedia Performances:** Xenakis created large-scale multimedia works called polytopes, which combined music with light, laser beams, and floodlights to form a total artistic experience.

- His theoretical writings, such as the book _Formalized Music: Thought and Mathematics in Composition_ (1971), remain influential in contemporary music theory.

## **I. Genesis, Context, and Compositional Philosophy**

*Persephassa*, composed by Iannis Xenakis in 1969, stands as a cornerstone of twentieth-century percussion ensemble repertoire, defining a unique approach to rhythm, timbre, and spatial composition. The piece was commissioned jointly by the Office de Radiodiffusion Télévision Française (ORTF) and the Shiraz Arts Festival and premiered in the historically significant location of Persepolis, Iran, on September 9, 1969\.1 The title itself, a variant name for the goddess Persephone (Kore/Proserpina), reflects the composer's overarching preoccupation with elemental forces, telluric cycles, and the mathematical representation of life's multiplicity.1

The dedication of the work to **Les Percussions de Strasbourg** 1 is structurally important. Xenakis developed a close working relationship with the ensemble, which would later commission his other percussion masterpiece, *Pléïades* (1979).3 This collaborative history is not merely a historical footnote; it influences performance practice. Xenakis often granted his trusted dedicatees, such as those within Les Percussions de Strasbourg, a certain degree of interpretive openness regarding instrument choice and setup, a practice also observed in his solo works.5 Therefore, while the musical language is mathematically rigorous, the execution demands practical flexibility in instrument selection and arrangement, provided the acoustical intent—maximum contrast and clarity—is preserved.

### **The Mathematical Musician: Spatiality and Stochastic Logic**

Xenakis was an architect and engineer as well as a composer, and his mid-to-late 1960s compositional period focused heavily on treating **space as a fundamental musical parameter**.1 *Persephassa* is directly related to earlier spatial works like *Terretektorh* (1966), where the distinction between performer and audience is blurred or dissolved.1 The rhythmic content of the work is often generated through **rhythmic sieves (*cribles*)**, mathematically calculated rhythmic sequences used to distribute attacks.7 This notation reflects set-theoretical operations and permutations, providing a graphic conveyance of the mathematical processes underlying the rhythm.5

The core aesthetic result is the contrast between highly regular, mathematically derived patterns and stochastic "clouds" of sound, known as *nuages*, which are punctuated by sudden accents and moments of silence.1 The challenge for performers is to convey the underlying mathematical precision even when the resulting texture sounds chaotic or irregular. This focus on rhythmic structure, divorced from traditional melodic or harmonic concerns, reintroduces rhythm as a primary organizational principle in contemporary music.3

## **II. The Acoustic Battlefield: Spatialization and The Circle of Six**

The successful realization of *Persephassa* relies entirely on maximizing the acoustic and psychological impact of spatial organization. The central requirement is that the six percussionists be distributed around the audience, creating a circular sound field where the audience is seated amid the performers in a superimposed space.1

### **The Required Performance Environment and Setup**

The physical placement transforms the performance into a "sound choreography".3 Xenakis utilizes techniques derived from *musique concrète*, where the transfer of musical material between spatially separated groups creates the impression of movement.6 The listener's body becomes both the "receptacle and relay" of the music.9 This means the chosen venue is inherently functional; its acoustic properties (such as reverberation and projection) directly influence the composition. Practical performances have embraced novel spatial solutions, such as the 2010 performance presented on and around Central Park Lake, with listeners in rowboats, demonstrating that spatial novelty and immersion take precedence over conventional concert staging.1 If performed indoors, the setup must maximize the distance between players and ensure projection toward the center to fully envelop the acoustic field.

### **Utilizing Space as a Musical Parameter**

The dramatic impact of spatial distribution is most evident in passages where rhythmic figures or accents are rapidly **passed around the ensemble**.1 For this transfer to be effective, performers must select instruments (especially within categories like drums or wood blocks) that exhibit sufficient acoustic homogeneity so that the listener perceives the sound as one single moving waveform, rather than six disparate sources momentarily aligning.

A particularly demanding passage is the **enormous accelerando**.1 Xenakis builds up to six layers of spiraling patterns, resulting in rolled accents rotating around the six players every second, reaching a tempo of 360 beats per minute.1 The coordination challenge here is significant: the ensemble must synchronize the *start* of this acceleration but precisely stagger the continuous accents to create the auditory illusion of a continuous, swirling movement.

Furthermore, the work oscillates between strictly controlled rhythmic structures and moments of radical separation. During the *nuages*, players follow "independent temporal pathways" to form a chaotic rhythmic "cloud," or *nuage*.10 This is a moment of controlled temporal dissonance. The critical performance challenge is not just achieving synchronization when required, but also achieving a collective, deliberate disintegration of time when necessary, requiring exceptional ensemble trust and non-verbal communication in the absence of a central conductor.

## **III. Defining the Sonic Palette: Comprehensive Instrumentation and Setup**

*Persephassa* requires a substantial and carefully selected arsenal of instruments across six identical multi-percussion stations.1 The instrumentation relies on indefinite pitched and semi-pitched sources, demanding a focus on timbre, attack, and decay.

### **Instrumentation Categorization and Requirement Details**

Xenakis calls for a wide range of specific sound sources, including drums, wood blocks, cymbals, gongs, and sound effects.1

1. **Drums (Peaux):** An "arsenal of drums" (membranophones) is specified.1 Performers must select drums based on achieving maximum tonal separation and clarity for the dense rhythmic material. When executing fast, high-density rolls (such as those at 360 BPM), the drum heads must provide immediate, clear articulation and good rebound.1 Techniques such as playing the bass drum flat or angled to allow two-beater technique are necessary.11 Furthermore, efficient muffling, often achieved by clipping a towel to the rim, is crucial for controlling decay, especially during rapid, non-sustained attacks.11

2. **Wood Instruments (Simantras):** A key requirement is the use of wood blocks, referred to as ***simantras***.1 This designation is highly specific. *Simantras* are traditionally wooden planks or blocks that produce a dry, sharp, and penetrating sound.5 It is essential for ensembles performing *Persephassa* to use instruments that maximize the direct "contact sound" rather than instruments with resonant fundamental pitches. This dry, articulate timbre distinguishes *Persephassa* sharply from Xenakis’s later work, *Pléïades*, which famously employs the metallic, pitched *sixxen*.3 The sonic identity of *Persephassa* is thus rooted in the dry, articulate wood sound, which is necessary for accurately rendering the rhythmic sieves; too much resonance would blur the mathematical intricacy of the patterns. Notational analysis also indicates that complex multi-mallet techniques, such as holding four mallets (two over, two under) on the blocks, may be required for maximum attack density.5

3. **Metals (Cymbals and Gongs):** Cymbals and gongs are used to provide extreme dynamic and timbral contrast.1 Gongs must be capable of projection across the dynamic spectrum, from the quietest sounds to the "most explosive forte".13 Cymbals require mastery of specific techniques for short, choked crashes (muffling against the body) and for achieving extremely soft, sustained crashes (holding the cymbals nearly parallel and moving only the top plate).11

4. **Effects and Small Percussion:** The score requires miscellaneous sound effects, including sirens, maracas, and pebbles.1 These contribute primarily to the textural, stochastic *nuages* or serve as abrupt, non-musical interjections.

### **Setup Strategies for Multi-Percussion Efficiency**

The fundamental challenge is integrating these disparate instruments into six identical and physically separated multi-percussion setups. Given the demands for rapid transitions and complex sticking (potentially four mallets on the simantras), the arrangement of instruments must be highly efficient, minimizing unnecessary movement.5

## **IV. Rhythmic Execution and Notational Interpretation**

Xenakis’s notation demands an approach focused on clarity, density, and exaggerated contrast. The score's graphic presentation facilitates the composer's processes (rhythmic sieves, permutations) 5, but performers must understand the interpretive implications of these techniques.

### **Interpreting Rhythmic Sieves (*Cribles*) and Temporal Dissonance**

The rhythmic structures are often characterized by a "static rhythmical concept" that dictates the flow of attacks.5 When executing the sieve patterns, rhythmic precision is paramount, as the clarity of these mathematical relationships defines the structure of the piece.

The most difficult challenge lies in the execution of the *nuages*. Here, players abandon the central pulse and follow "independent temporal pathways," employing "maximally dense and non-periodic rhythms" to form a chaotic, six-part sonic "cloud".10 This deliberate move into temporal dissonance requires sophisticated ensemble coordination; the chaos is not random but a shared, structured disintegration of time, creating a "tempest of non-aligned notes".10 The ensemble must rehearse the exact moment of rhythmic dissolution and the subsequent return to strict coordination.

### **The Nuance of Dynamic Accents**

Dynamics in *Persephassa* are used to provide "irregular punctuation" 5 and introduce or interject new ideas.5 Xenakis specifically required that accents must **"speak amid the cacophony"**.5 This principle necessitates a strategy of dynamic relativity rather than absolute volume. Analysis of Xenakis's instructions indicates that performers may need to play the underlying, non-accented material softer than initially indicated in the score to ensure the prominence of the accents.5 The accents must be executed with a sense of sudden, even "savage" dynamic distribution, serving to delineate the underlying structure from the dense texture.13 For a listener, these dynamic events function as the defining rhythmic and structural "pitches" of the piece.5

## **V. Practical Preparation: Mallets, Mufflings, and Rehearsal Techniques**

The complexity of *Persephassa* requires meticulous practical preparation, particularly in customizing the mallet selection and developing spatial rehearsal techniques.

### **Mallet Selection Philosophy: Beyond Hardness**

Mallet selection must extend beyond the simple binary of "hard" or "soft." The primary consideration should be the **articulation**—the quality of the attack, which is the most controllable element for percussionists.15 Mallets should be chosen based on the desired "attack" characteristics, using descriptions typically applied to wind playing, such as "sharp," "dark," "heavy," or "bright," rather than just material hardness.15

A crucial factor for execution in a spatially distributed performance is **projection**. Studies suggest that relying solely on hardness to achieve projection can lead to poor technique in softer passages. Therefore, mallets with sufficient **weight** (approximately 38 to 46 grams) are recommended, as they generate a fuller sound and allow the performer to remain physically relaxed while meeting the required dynamic levels.16 Heavy mallets project sound more effectively across the necessary distance without requiring the performer to strike the instrument harder, which is essential when sustaining high velocities and dynamics, such as in the 360 BPM sections.16

Instrument-specific selection includes using hard, unwrapped mallets (rubber, plastic, or wood) for the *simantras* to ensure a dry, sharp contact sound crucial for rhythmic clarity.11 For gongs and large drums, heavy felt mallets are necessary to maximize fundamental tone and control sustain.14 Given the rapid changes and demands of the score, the percussionist must prepare an extensive and highly organized selection of beaters, treated as a "pallet" of sound colors.15

### **Efficient Practice and Rehearsal Strategies**

The performance requires specialized rehearsal techniques to overcome the acoustic and logistical challenges of spatialization.

First, **acoustic testing in the performance venue** is mandatory.6 Since the sound envelope and the spiraling effect are structural components of the work, the ensemble must assess how material transfers spatially in the intended hall, adjusting individual dynamics and setup angles based on real-time feedback.

Second, synchronization relies heavily on peripheral awareness. Because the physical separation complicates the use of a traditional conductor, performers must emphasize highly developed **auditory and visual tracking** of their immediate neighbors, particularly when rapid accents or imitative rhythms are being passed around the circle.1

Finally, the rehearsal process must involve a pedagogical analysis of Xenakis’s structure. While Xenakis granted interpretive freedom, systematic studies of his notation (such as those published in *Percussive Notes* by Jan Williams 5) underscore the value of a methodical approach to setup and sticking choices. This structured preparation is necessary to master the precise mathematics underlying the seemingly chaotic surface of the work.

## **VI. Conclusion: Legacy and Final Performance Considerations**

*Persephassa* remains one of the most intellectually rigorous and physically demanding works in the percussion repertoire. Its importance lies not only in its relentless rhythmic complexity but in its pioneering use of space as a core structural element, forcing the audience into a deeply immersive, physical experience.

Successful performance requires adherence to three main principles:

1. **Prioritize Spatial Movement:** The setup must maximize the distance between the six players to ensure the perception of sound transfer and rotation, making the audience the center of the acoustic event.1  
2. **Master Rhythmic Dissonance and Clarity:** The ensemble must achieve extreme technical control to execute the high-speed sieve patterns and the calculated transition into and out of the stochastic *nuages*.1 The rhythmic events serve as "irregular punctuation" and must be surgically precise.5  
3. **Exaggerate Dynamic Contrast:** The sound sources must be selected for maximum articulation (dry woods, precise attack) and projection (heavy mallets). Accents must be violently or savagely delineated from the surrounding texture to fulfill their structural role of speaking "amid the cacophony".5

By approaching *Persephassa* not merely as a rhythmic exercise but as a spatial and architectural acoustic phenomenon, modern ensembles can realize Xenakis's vision and convey the explosive energy contained within his complex mathematical structures.3
